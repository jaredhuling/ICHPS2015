Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm




  • Dilation of large artery in abdominal cavity

    • More common among elderly, especially men and smokers
    • Life-threatening, due to increased chance of aortic rupture

  • Standard Surgical Procedures

    • High mortality associated with surgical repair in rupture cases
    • Open Repair, conventional, more invasive, long recovery
    • Endovascular Repair, more recent, less invasive

Previous Analysis




  • Randomized Clinical Trials

    • Suggest short term reduction in mortality for endo, 1 little difference after 3 years 2

  • Issues

    • Short-term follow-up 1
    • Small One of the largest long-term controlled studies only had 10 AAA-related deaths 2










  1. A Randomized Trial Comparing Conventional and Endovascular Repair of Abdominal Aortic Aneurysms , Prinssen et al., 2004. New England Journal of Medicine, volume 351, number 16, 2004, pp. 1607-1618
  2. Long-Term Comparison of Endovascular and Open Repair of Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm , Lederle et al., 2012. New England Journal of Medicine, volume 367, number 21, 2012, pp. 1988-1997

Previous Analysis




  • Medicare Enrollment Data (observational)

    • Lots of it 128k patients with long-term follow-up
    • ~44k deaths
  • Propensity-score-matched cohorts 1

    • Potentially differential benefit for various subgroups
    • Suggests older patients benefit more from endovascular repair
    • Reinterventions more common after endovascular repair
  • Issues

    • Cannot control for everything; many factors that cannot be measured determine type of repair performed

Instrumental Variables

Can we estimate the causal effect of $X$ on $T$ without knowing $U$?

Consider the model $T = X^T\beta + \epsilon$. In the confounding case, $U$ is contained in $\epsilon$

so $\hat{\beta}_{OLS} = \beta + (X^TX)^{-1}X^T\epsilon$ is biased. $E(X^T\epsilon) \neq 0$

Instrumental Variables




  • Roughly speaking, we can write

    $Association(Z, T) = Association(Z, X) \times Association(X, T)$

    and solve for $Association(X, T)$

    • In the simple linear model, $\hat{\beta}_{IV} = cov(T, Z) / cov(X, Z)$ is consistent for $\beta$

    • (under some assumptions)

  • We can think of the instrument $Z$ as an experimental handle

    • Considering $X$ to be a treatment, when $Z$ is changed, the treatment status is changed

    • Through and only through this change, the observed outcome $T$ is changed

Instrumental Variables




  • Example from Permut and Hebel (1989)

    • Interest in the effect of smoking of pregnant mother on child's birthweight
    • The IV is the randomized encouragement of mothers to stop smoking
    • Intent-to-Treat estimator: the effect on encouragement to quit smoking on child's birthweight
    • However, we are interested in the effect of smoking on child's birthweight
    • ITT estimator: encouragement results in an average increase in birthweight of 98g
      • Difference in fraction of those who quit smoking for those who were encouraged to quit vs
      those who were not: 0.23 (effect of IV on treatment status)

    • IV estimator: quitting smoking results in an average increase in birthweight of

      $98g/0.23 = 430g$

Instrumental Variables in Linear Regression


  • Assumptions

    • model:
    •      $T = \beta X + U $
    • where
    •      $Z\perp\!\!\!\perp U$
    •      $T\perp\!\!\!\perp Z|X, U$
    •      $X \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Z$
  • Estimator

    •      $\hat{\beta}_{IV}$    $ = (Z'X)^{-1}Z'T$ is consistent whereas $\hat{\beta}_{OLS}$ is not
      $\hat{\beta}_{2SLS} = (\hat{X}'\hat{X})^{-1}\hat{X}'T$ , where $\hat{X} = Z(Z'Z)^{-1}Z'X$ is another consistent IV estimator
  • Interpretation as Two-stage least squares:

    •     Equivalent to regressing $X$ on $Z$ and then
      regressing $T$ on $\hat{X}$ from the first regression

Instrumental Variables in Survival Analysis




  • Challenges

    • Traditional IV methods require linearity, yet the Cox proportional hazards model is nonlinear

    • Parametric models are too restrictive for survival data in medical settings, rendering most structural equation modelling approaches inappropriate

    • Methods that do not require linearity typically require other strong assumptions, such as no measurement error for the endogenous variable $X$

Instrumental Variables in Survival Analysis




  • Existing work for IV methods for survival data

    • Most work relies on fully specified parametric structural equation models: Tang and Lee (1998), Muthen and Masyn (2005), and Chen, et al (2011)

    • Two-stage residual inclusion 1 allows for consistent estimation for nonlinear parametric models with no censoring

    • A recent method utilizes an additive hazards model 2 which alleviates some of the restrictions of fully parametric approaches









  1. Two-stage Residual Inclusion Estimation: Addressing Endogeneity in Health Econometric Modeling, Joseph Terza, Anirban Basu, Paul Rathouz. J. Health Econ., 27, 2008, pp. 531-543.
  2. Instrumental Variable Additive Hazards Models, Jialiang Li, Jason Fine, Alan Brookhart. Biometrics, 71, 2015, pp. 122-130.

Instrumental Variables in Survival Analysis


  • Instrumental Variable Additive Hazards

    • Model for the hazard function: \begin{align*} h(t; X, X_o, U) &= h_0(t) + \beta X + \boldsymbol\beta_o'\mathbf{X_o} + \boldsymbol\beta_u' \mathbf{U} \\ & \\ X &= \alpha_c + \alpha_Z Z + \boldsymbol\alpha_o'\mathbf{X}_o + \boldsymbol\alpha_u'\mathbf{U} + \epsilon \end{align*}
    • Fit linear model for $X$ on $Z$ and $\mathbf{X_o}$ and use $\hat{X}$ in additive hazards model

    • Requires linearity of the effect of the instrument on the endogenous variable $X$
  • Two-stage Residual Inclusion

    • Requires the model for the endogenous variable to have no error

    • Unclear if it applies with the occurrence of censoring

Accelerated Failure Time Model





  • Standard Semiparametric AFT Model

    • $\log \widetilde{T}_i = \beta X_i + \epsilon_i$, $i=1,\dots,n$

      where $\epsilon_i$ come from a common, completely unspecified distribution and $\epsilon_i \perp\!\!\!\perp (X_i, C_i)$

      Let $C_i$ be the censoring time for subject $i$, where $C_i \perp\!\!\!\perp \widetilde{T}_i | X_i$

      We observe $(T_i, \Delta_i, X_i)$, where $ T_i = \widetilde{T}_i \wedge C_i $ and $ \Delta_i = I(\widetilde{T}_i \leq C_i) $

    • Nice interpretation: $\log$ of the survival time is linear in the covariates

    • Linearity provides opportunity for use of IVs

Accelerated Failure Time Model




Accelerated Failure Time Model - Estimation




Rank-based Estimation of $\beta$ \begin{align*} U_n(\beta) &= \sum_{i = 1}^n\int \rho(t, \beta)\{ X_i - \overline{X}(t, \beta) \} \mbox{ } \mathrm{d}N_i(t, \beta) \\ & \\ \mbox{where } \overline{X}(t, \beta) &\equiv \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n X_j I(\epsilon_i^\beta \ge t) \mbox{ } / \mbox{ } \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n I(\epsilon_i^\beta \ge t) \mbox{ and} \\ & \\ \epsilon_i^\beta &= \log{T_i} - \beta X_i \mbox{ is the residual for subject }i \mbox{ and } N_i(t, \beta) = I(\epsilon_i^\beta \leq t, \Delta_i = 1) \end{align*}

$\hat{\beta}$ is the zero crossing of $S_n(\beta)$. Its asymptotic normality was proved by Tsiatis (1990) and Ying (1993)

Accelerated Failure Time Model - Estimation




Rank-based Estimation of $\beta$ \begin{align*} U_n(\beta) = \sum_{i = 1}^n \rho(\log T_i - \beta X_i, \beta)\left\{ {X_j} - \frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n {X_j}I(\log T_j - \beta X_j \ge \log T_i - \beta X_i)}{ \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^nI(\log T_j - \beta X_j \ge \log T_i - \beta X_i)} \right\} \Delta_i \end{align*}

  • Computational Challenges
    • $S_n(\beta)$ is neither continuous nor monotone
    • Continuous approximations to $S_n(\beta)$
    • To ensure monotonicity, let $\rho(t, \beta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n I(\epsilon_j^\beta \ge t)$

Accelerated Failure Time Model - Instrumental Variables




Assumptions for our methodology




    • The underlying model is:

      $\log \widetilde{T}_i = \beta X_i + U_i$,
      $i=1,\dots,n$

      • where
      •      $Z_i\perp\!\!\!\perp U_i$
      •      $\widetilde{T}_i\perp\!\!\!\perp Z_i|X_i, U_i$
      •      $X_i \not\!\perp\!\!\!\perp Z_i$

      • a key difference from standard AFT assumptions:
      •      $C_i\perp\!\!\!\perp (X_i, Z_i, U_i, \widetilde{T}_i)$

Methods for Unmeasured Confounders - possible estimator of $\beta$




In the spirit of IV, replace $X$ with ${\color{Yellow}Z}$ \begin{align*} U_n^{IV}(\beta) &= \sum_{i = 1}^n\int \rho(t, \beta)\{ {\color{Yellow}Z_i} - {\color{Yellow}\overline{Z}(t, \beta)} \} \mbox{ } \mathrm{d}N_i(t, \beta) \\ & \\ \mbox{where } {\color{Yellow}\overline{Z}(t, \beta)} &\equiv \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n {\color{Yellow}Z_j} I(\epsilon_i^\beta \ge t) \mbox{ } / \mbox{ } \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n I(\epsilon_i^\beta \ge t) \mbox{ and} \\ & \\ \epsilon_i^\beta &= \log{T_i} - \beta {\color{Yellow}X_i} \mbox{ is the residual for subject }i \mbox{ and } N_i(t, \beta) = I(\epsilon_i^\beta \leq t, \Delta_i = 1) \end{align*}

We compare ${\color{Yellow}Z_i}$ with ${\color{Yellow}\overline{Z}(t, \beta)}$, the mean IV value for those in the risk set for $i$

Methods for Unmeasured Confounders - possible estimator of $\beta$




However, in
\begin{align*} U_n^{IV}(\beta) &= \sum_{i = 1}^n\int \rho(t, \beta)\{ {\color{Yellow}Z_i} - {\color{Yellow}\overline{Z}(t, \beta)} \} \mbox{ } \mathrm{d}N_i(t, \beta) \\ & \mbox{ } \\ \epsilon_i^\beta &= \log{T_i} - \beta {\color{Yellow}X_i} \mbox{ depends on } {\color{Yellow} U_i} \end{align*}

and thus this estimator is not consistent for $\beta$

Methods for Unmeasured Confounders - possible estimator of $\beta$




In the spirit of 2SLS, replace $X$ with ${\color{Yellow}\hat{X}} = Z(Z'Z)^{-1}Z'X$ \begin{align*} U_n^{2SLS}(\beta) &= \sum_{i = 1}^n\int \rho(t, \beta)\{ {\color{Yellow}\hat{X}_i} - {\color{Yellow}\hat{\overline{X}}(t, \beta)} \} \mbox{ } \mathrm{d}N_i(t, \beta) \\ & \\ \mbox{where } {\color{Yellow}\hat{\overline{X}}(t, \beta)} &\equiv \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n {\color{Yellow}\hat{X}_j} I({\color{Yellow}\hat{\epsilon}_i^\beta} \ge t) \mbox{ } / \mbox{ } \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n I( {\color{Yellow}\hat{\epsilon}_i^\beta} \ge t) \mbox{ and} \\ & \\ {\color{Yellow}\hat{\epsilon}_i^\beta} &= \log{T_i} - \beta {\color{Yellow}\hat{X}_i} \mbox{ is the residual for subject }i \mbox{ and } N_i(t, \beta) = I({\color{Yellow}\hat{\epsilon}_i^\beta} \leq t, \Delta_i = 1) \end{align*}

However there is no theoretical basis for this and it imposes a linear model

assumption on the effect of the IV on $X$

Methods for Unmeasured Confounders




Replacing $X$ with $Z$ causes censoring to become non-ignorable

A natural method for handling this is inverse probability-of-censoring weighting \begin{align*} U_n^{IV-IPCW}(\beta) &= \sum_{i = 1}^n\int \rho(t, \beta)\{ {\color{Yellow}Z_i} - {\color{Yellow}\overline{Z}_{G_C}(t, \beta)} \} \frac{\mathrm{d}N_i(t, \beta)}{G_C(t + \beta X_i)} \\ & \\ \mbox{where } {\color{Yellow}\overline{Z}_{G_C}(t, \beta)} &\equiv \frac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{{\color{Yellow}Z_j} I({\color{Yellow}\epsilon_i^\beta} \ge t)}{G_C(t + \beta X_j)} \mbox{ } / \mbox{ } \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{I( {\color{Yellow}\epsilon_i^\beta} \ge t)} {G_C(t + \beta X_j)} \mbox{ and} \\ & \\ {\color{Yellow}\epsilon_i^\beta} &= \log{T_i} - \beta {\color{Yellow}X_i} \mbox{ is the residual for subject }i \mbox{ and } N_i(t, \beta) = I({\color{Yellow}\epsilon_i^\beta} \leq t, \Delta_i = 1)\\ & \\ \mbox{and } G_C & \mbox{ is the Kaplan-Meier estimator for the survival function of } C \end{align*}

Methods for Unmeasured Confounders - Inference




  • Full bootstrap: solve $n^{-1/2}U_n^{IV-IPCW^*}(\beta)$ $\mbox{ }B$ times, where $n^{-1/2}U_n^{IV-IPCW^*}(\beta)$
    is the estimating equation based on resampled data

    • However, due to the computational difficulties in solving $n^{-1/2}U_n^{IV-IPCW}(\beta)$,
      this is impractical

    • Instead, if we can relate the variance of $n^{-1/2}U_n^{IV-IPCW}(\beta)$ to the variance of
      $n^{1/2}(\hat{\beta} - \beta_0)$, a greatly less computationally demanding procedure can be developed

  • This motivates the use of resampling strategies from Zeng and Lin (2008)

    • Instead of solving estimating equations, involves evaluating perturbed equations

    • Can accomodate nonsmooth estimating equations/functions

Standard AFT - Inference




  • For the standard AFT model, under some regularity conditions (Parzen et al. 1994, Zeng and Lin 2008):

    • Uniformly in a neighborhood of $\beta_0$,

      $n^{-1/2}U_n(\beta) = n^{-1/2}\sum_{i=1}^n S_i(\beta_0) + A n^{1/2}(\beta - \beta_0) + o_p(1+n^{1/2}||\beta - \beta_0||)$
      where $A$ is the asymptotic slope of $\frac{1}{n}U_n(\beta_0)$

    • Hence, $n^{1/2}(\hat{\beta} - \beta_0)$ is asymptotically norm with variance $\Sigma = A^{-1}V(A^{-1})^T$, where $V = \lim_{n \to \infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^nS_i S_i^T$

    • (Zeng and Lin 2008) proposed to estimate $A$ by regressing the following perturbation $n^{-1/2}U_n(\hat{\beta} + n^{-1/2}Z_b),\mbox{ }Z_b \sim N(0,1)$ on $Z_b, b=1,\dots,B$ and to estimate $V$ by taking the sample variance of $B$ resampled equations $n^{-1/2}U_n^{*}(\hat{\beta})$

Standard AFT - Inference




  • Zeng and Lin's justification estimates for $A$ and $V$:

    • Recall: (Zeng and Lin 2008) proposed to estimate $A$ by regressing the following perturbation
      $n^{-1/2}U_n(\hat{\beta} + n^{-1/2}Z_b)$ on $Z_b, b=1,\dots,B$ and to estimate $V$ by taking the sample variance
      of $B$ resampled equations $n^{-1/2}U_n^{*}(\hat{\beta})$

    • $n^{-1/2}U_n^{*}(\hat{\beta}) = n^{-1/2}U_n^{*}(\hat{\beta}) - n^{-1/2}U_n(\hat{\beta}) = n^{-1/2}\sum_{i=1}^n(M_i - 1)S_i(\beta_0) + o_p(1+n^{1/2}||\hat{\beta} - \beta_0||)$
      where $M_i$ is the number of times the $ith$ observation appears in the bootstrap sample

    • $ n^{-1/2}U_n(\hat{\beta} + n^{-1/2}Z) = n^{-1/2}U_n(\hat{\beta} + n^{-1/2}Z) - n^{-1/2}U_n(\hat{\beta})$
      $\mbox{ }= An^{1/2}(\hat{\beta} + n^{-1/2}Z - \hat{\beta}) + o_p(1)= AZ + o_p(1)$
  • However, all of this does not apply directly for us, because

    • $ n^{-1/2}U_n^{IV-IPCW}(\beta) $ is a function of $\hat{G}_C$

AFT-IV-IPCW - Inference

  • Instead, we have
    • \begin{align*} n^{-1/2}U_n^{IV-IPCW}(\beta, \hat{G}_C) &= n^{-1/2}\sum_{i=1}^nS_i(\beta_0, \hat{G}_C) + An^{1/2}(\beta - \beta_0) + o_p(1 + n^{1/2}||\beta - \beta_0 ||) \\ \mbox{and if }&\approx n^{-1/2}\sum_{i=1}^nS_i(\beta_0, G_C) + An^{1/2}(\beta - \beta_0) + An^{1/2}(\hat{G}_C - G_C) \\ & + o_p(1 + n^{1/2}||\beta - \beta_0 || + n^{1/2}||\hat{G}_C - G_C ||_\infty)\\ & \\ \mbox{then the asymptotic } &\mbox{variance of } n^{1/2}(\hat{\beta} - \beta_0) \mbox{ is } A_1^{-1}\left\{ V + A_2\Sigma_G A_2^T \right\}(A_1^{-1})^T \\ \mbox{ where } \Sigma_G &= \lim_{n \to \infty}Var( \sum_{i=1}^n\Delta_i\hat{G}_C(\log T_i) ) %\\ %\mbox{ or } A^{-1} &\left( \lim_{n \to \infty}Var\{ n^{-1/2}\sum_{i=1}^nS_i'(\beta_0,G_C(\log{T_i}))\Delta_i (\hat{G}_C(\log T_i) - G_C(\log T_i) )\} \right) (A^{-1})^T \\ \end{align*}

Preliminary Analysis




  • Data

    • Rupture cases (2853 patients)
    • ~66% censoring
    • 9 years of follow-up
    • 962 deaths, 733 of which were related to endo repair
    • Instrument: the proportion of endovascular AAA surgeries by institution in the past 365 days
    • Standard methods to determine instrument strength support the use of this as an instrument

  • Methods

    • Standard AFT model, AFT model with IV replacement, AFT-2SLS
    • Bootstrap for confidence intervals
    • Due to time considerations AFT model with IV and inverse weighting was not used

Conclusions




  • AAA Rupture Data

    • Standard AFT model without consideration for confounding suggests slight benefit from endo
    • AFT-IV / 2SLS models suggest potential benefit from open surgery

  • Estimators

    • Standard AFT model exhibits clear bias in the presence of confounding
    • AFT-IV is not consistent, but in practice is much better than AFT
    • IPCW provides a theoretically sound IV method
